# What Are Learners Actually Committed To? Commitment as a Context for Learning
{: .no_toc }

{ A field inquiry from project-based leadership education—and a question I am still pursuing | fs-6 }

[نسخه‌ی فارسی](/thinking/research-notes/commitment-context-learning-fa)

{: .note-title }
> About this note
>
> This is a working research and field note, not a scientific paper or a claim of causal proof. It grew from aggregated observations across several project-based leadership programs, including large cohorts of roughly 200–350 participants and a year-long program of approximately 30–40 participants that met twice each week for two-hour sessions. The programs were not designed as controlled studies. There was no random assignment, validated predeclared outcome measure, blinded assessment, or comparison group.
>
> I was also the facilitator who helped participants clarify their projects, asked the questions described here, and observed what followed. That dual role creates obvious risks of expectancy, selection, and interpretation bias. Participant details are omitted and the account is intentionally presented at group level. The published research discussed later offers adjacent concepts; it does not prove my interpretation of these programs.

In project-based leadership education, I repeatedly saw people in the same room encounter what appeared to be the same course in radically different ways. Some participants seemed to enter the world of their projects. They listened as though something real was at stake, searched beyond the assigned material, asked sharper questions, used one another as resources, and continued working through breakdowns. Others attended, planned, wrote, tried, or appeared active, yet the course seemed to remain outside their lives.

That difference left me with a question I have not been able—or willing—to put down:

> **What are learners actually committed to, and how might the object of that commitment shape what becomes available for them to learn?**

## The question began with a visible difference

The leadership programs were project based. Participants did not only discuss leadership as a concept; they took responsibility for projects and were expected to act in the world. Over time, I noticed a recurring difference.

Some people remained with their projects until the end of the program. Their projects were not always easy or successful, but the participants kept returning to the work. They seemed to understand the course more deeply. They did not merely remember its vocabulary. They used the course to see, question, coordinate, decide, and act. It was as though they were living inside the inquiry rather than studying it from a distance.

Other participants were less engaged with the realization of their projects. They might attend regularly, take notes, complete a form, make a plan, or perform an initial attempt. Yet the material appeared to have less consequence for how they acted. I began to wonder whether the difference was not only ability, intelligence, discipline, or the quality of instruction. Perhaps commitment was helping organize the learning itself.

The observation also made me cautious about equating intelligence, admission to a highly selective institution, or access to excellent teaching with deep learning or professional excellence. Those conditions can expand capacity and opportunity. They do not by themselves determine which future organizes a learner’s attention or what that learner will accept as sufficient completion.

At first, that thought was too broad. Everyone could say, sincerely, “I am committed to my project” or “I want to learn.” The useful question became more precise:

> **Committed to what, exactly?**

## The setting in which I examined the question more deliberately

The initial question arose across large leadership courses, some with approximately 200, 300, or 350 participants. I examined it more deliberately in a smaller, year-long program with roughly 30–40 people. We met twice a week for two hours. Participants brought projects, and for weeks we worked to make those projects clearer.

The group did not operate as a collection of isolated students. Participants looked at one another’s projects, questioned assumptions, offered ideas, and learned from problems that were not their own. I followed the projects over time, not only the participants’ classroom contributions.

I also introduced a form containing a deliberately simple question:

> **What is your commitment in relation to your project?**

The question was not a request for a slogan. It was the beginning of a conversation. As people explained their answers, different objects of commitment became visible.

One person was committed to completing the project. Another was committed to trying. Another was committed to planning. Another wanted to find out what was going on. Someone wanted to discover whether the project was possible. Someone else was committed to writing enough of the project to qualify for participation in the class. Another seemed organized around showing that they were making an effort.

Some answers even contained nested layers: planning to try, trying to become willing, or wanting to prepare to act. Each layer could be sincere, yet each could move the criterion of completion farther from the actual project.

These were not necessarily dishonest answers. In many cases, people had never been invited to distinguish among them.

## The distinction: commitment is not one thing

The most important distinction for me was not between “committed people” and “uncommitted people.” Almost everyone was committed to something. The difference was **what their actions were organized to complete**.

| Possible object of commitment | What can count as completion | A possible response to a breakdown |
|---|---|---|
| Realizing the project or its intended result | Credible evidence that the result exists, or a responsible decision to redesign, pivot, or stop | Search, learn, ask, revise, coordinate, and test another path |
| Trying | A sincere or sufficient effort has been made | “I tried; it did not work” can close the inquiry |
| Planning | A plan has been produced or improved | Return to the plan, sometimes without entering execution |
| Wanting | The desire has been expressed and preserved | Repeat or defend the intention |
| Compliance or access | The form, proposal, or required artifact has been submitted | Optimize for the requirement rather than the underlying future |
| Appearing active or proving effort | Other people can see evidence of activity | Invest in visible signals of work |
| Determining feasibility | A conclusion of “possible” or “not possible” has been reached | Complete the investigation without realizing the project |

These categories can overlap. A participant may be committed to planning **in order to** act, or to trying **as part of** a serious search for a workable path. Planning and effort are not inferior activities; every substantial project requires them. The problem appears when an intermediate activity becomes the final object of responsibility. Then a person can complete the plan, complete the attempt, or complete the performance of effort while the intended future remains unrealized.

This is also why the strength of commitment is not the only question. A person can be intensely committed to looking competent, complying with a course rule, or proving that a project is impossible. The inquiry must ask both:

1. **How strong is the commitment?**
2. **To what is the commitment attached?**

## People often produced the condition they were organized to produce

My recurring observation was simple and unsettling: people often seemed to produce precisely the condition to which they were operationally committed.

Those committed to trying usually tried. When the attempt failed, they could honestly say that they had fulfilled their commitment. Those committed to planning often developed a plan. Those committed to learning whether something was possible often reached a conclusion about possibility. Those committed to appearing active could generate visible activity.

The result was not necessarily the project.

By contrast, when some participants shifted from “I will try,” “I will prepare,” or “I will see whether it can be done” toward “I am responsible for bringing this result into existence,” their relationship to the course appeared to change. They did not simply work longer. They listened differently. A concept in class could become a resource for a live problem. A peer’s experience could become relevant. An article, book, web search, expert, or difficult conversation could become part of the path.

This does not mean that commitment guaranteed success. Some projects should be redesigned. Some should stop. Conditions can make an outcome impossible, harmful, illegal, or no longer worthwhile. In the conversations, people sometimes described the shift as becoming committed to the result “at any cost.” I would not use that phrase literally. Responsible commitment is bounded by ethics, health, law, evidence, relationships, and limited resources. Sometimes stopping a particular project is the most faithful action toward the larger commitment it was meant to serve.

The distinction is not stubbornness versus quitting. It is whether a breakdown ends responsibility automatically or becomes new information for responsible choice.

## Commitment as a world in which the learner lives

The experience led me to think of commitment not only as a preference or internal quantity, but as a **context**.

A context is the background from which a situation shows up as meaningful. We normally do not see the context itself. We see the world it makes available. When a participant was genuinely answerable for realizing a project, the course occurred differently:

- a lecture could occur as a source of action rather than information to remember;
- confusion could occur as a question to investigate rather than evidence of inadequacy;
- a peer could occur as a collaborator or source rather than a competitor;
- feedback could occur as useful data rather than a judgment of identity;
- a breakdown could occur as a design problem rather than a verdict;
- and not knowing could occur as the beginning of inquiry rather than something to hide.

In that sense, the learner did not merely “have” a commitment. The learner **lived in a world constituted by it**.

This is the closest language I have found for what I observed. The external course content could remain broadly the same while its relevance, urgency, and possibilities changed. The future to which a person was answerable appeared to organize the present: what they noticed, what they ignored, whom they asked, how long they stayed with a difficulty, and what counted as a satisfactory answer.

I am using “context,” “world,” and “occurring” here as interpretive language influenced by ontological and phenomenological approaches to leadership. I am not presenting these terms as an experimentally established mechanism.

## What appeared to change in learning

When commitment was clearer and attached to realizing a meaningful result, I observed several changes in behavior. They were not measured with validated instruments, so I state them as observations rather than outcomes.

### Attention became selective in a useful way

Participants seemed to listen with a live question. They did not need every sentence to be generally interesting. They were alert to anything that could alter the project. The commitment gave attention a direction.

### Questions became more consequential

Questions moved from “What does this concept mean?” toward “What does this reveal about my project?” and “What action follows?” Conceptual understanding still mattered, but it became connected to a result for which the learner felt responsible.

### Resource seeking expanded

When the assigned material was insufficient, some participants searched for articles, books, examples, specialists, and other people’s experience. The boundary of the course became more permeable. Learning was no longer limited to what the instructor had prepared.

### Help seeking became easier

A person committed to protecting an image may experience asking for help as exposure. A person committed to the project may experience help as one more available resource. I observed participants becoming more willing to say, “I do not know,” because preserving the appearance of knowing was no longer the final purpose.

### Peer learning became operational

Looking at another person’s project was not merely an exercise in generosity. It exposed patterns, options, and breakdowns that could illuminate one’s own work. The group could become part of the learning system.

### Breakdowns generated inquiry

When the commitment was to effort, a failed attempt could complete the agreement: the effort had been made. When the commitment was to realization, the same failure more often generated another question: What is missing? Which assumption failed? Who needs to be involved? What must be learned? Does the design—or the commitment itself—need revision?

These patterns suggest a possible relationship among commitment, attention, self-regulation, and learning. They do not yet establish one.

## The educator’s leadership work: make the operative commitment visible

An educator, coach, or facilitator cannot manufacture another person’s commitment by command. A forced declaration may produce compliance, impression management, or resistance. The learner must retain authorship and the freedom to revise or refuse a commitment.

The educator can, however, create a distinction that was previously unavailable. The question “What is your commitment?” can make the background visible enough to examine.

I now see at least three parts to this leadership work.

### 1. Distinguish the declared goal from the operative commitment

A learner may declare, “I want to complete the project,” while their action is organized around avoiding embarrassment, satisfying the instructor, proving intelligence, or demonstrating effort. This is not necessarily hypocrisy. Human beings often act from contexts they have not named.

The task is not to accuse the learner of having a hidden motive. It is to help them observe what their current pattern of action reliably completes.

### 2. Create a choice rather than prescribe the correct answer

Once the distinction is visible, the learner can ask whether the present commitment is the one they choose. They may recommit to the result, narrow the project, redefine the result, decide that feasibility is the proper question, or leave the project responsibly. Awareness creates the possibility of authorship.

### 3. Revisit commitment throughout the work

Commitment is not a sentence written once at the beginning. It can weaken, split, migrate, or become obsolete as reality changes. Repeated conversation matters, especially after breakdowns. The educator can help the learner distinguish whether the obstacle calls for more effort, a different strategy, more knowledge, a new relationship, a redesigned project, or an honest completion.

The gift is not motivational pressure. It is the **distinction of commitment**: helping a person see the world from which they are already acting and recover the possibility of choosing it.

## A working model

The field observation can be expressed as a provisional sequence:

> **Object of commitment → context in which the project occurs → allocation of attention → standards for what counts as completion → inquiry, strategy, help seeking, and coordination → project action and opportunities for learning**

Several feedback loops are likely. Early progress may strengthen commitment. Failure may weaken it or deepen it. Peer recognition may change the object from learning to appearance. A clearer project may increase both commitment and self-regulation. Instructor attention may affect all of these.

The model therefore should not be read as a one-way causal chain. It is a map of questions:

- What future is organizing the learner’s present?
- What does the learner treat as sufficient completion?
- What becomes visible or invisible inside that context?
- Which learning behaviors follow?
- How do progress, feedback, identity, and relationships reshape the commitment over time?

## What adjacent research helps me see

The published literature contains several neighboring ideas. None is identical to the distinction developed in this note, and none validates my field account by itself.

### Goal commitment and goal-setting

Goal-setting research has long examined how specific and challenging goals, feedback, ability, task complexity, resources, and situational constraints relate to performance. Commitment is especially relevant when goals are difficult. Klein and colleagues also developed a concise measure of goal commitment, treating it as attachment and determination to reach a goal.

This literature helps clarify that commitment is not simply enthusiasm. It can be studied as a relationship to a goal. My field question adds another layer: before asking how committed someone is, we may need to identify **which goal their behavior is actually serving**.

### Achievement goals: learning, performing, and avoiding failure

Achievement-goal research distinguishes orientations such as developing competence, demonstrating competence, and avoiding the appearance of incompetence. These categories are not the same as my project distinctions, but they illuminate why two participants can perform similar activities from different organizing purposes. “Learning what is needed” and “showing that I am capable” can produce different responses to difficulty and feedback.

### Self-regulated learning

Research on self-regulated learning describes learners as active participants who set goals, choose strategies, monitor progress, manage motivation, seek help, and adapt. Many behaviors I observed—resource seeking, strategic revision, monitoring, and asking others—fit this neighboring framework.

The unresolved question is whether commitment to a meaningful result helps activate these behaviors, whether people who already possess strong self-regulation are more likely to make such commitments, or whether both are shaped by another factor.

### The quality and authorship of motivation

Self-determination theory distinguishes forms of motivation by their quality and degree of internalization, not only by their intensity. This matters because a participant can comply strongly with an instructor while lacking ownership of the project. A leadership intervention that makes commitment explicit must not become coercion. The learner’s autonomy, competence, relationships, and reasons for acting remain part of the picture.

### From intention to action

Research on implementation intentions shows how “if–then” plans can help translate goals into action. This is a useful corrective to any romantic idea that commitment alone is sufficient. A declared future without implementation, skill, feedback, and opportunity remains a declaration.

At the same time, planning is a bridge rather than necessarily the destination. A technically excellent plan can still serve the commitment to planning rather than the commitment to realizing the future.

### Ontological leadership as interpretive language

The ontological and phenomenological model of leadership distinguishes knowing about leadership from being and acting as a leader. It also gives language to context, the way situations occur, and commitment to a future as sources of action. This language helps me articulate the experience of “living in the project.”

I use it as a conceptual lens, not as causal evidence for the observations reported here.

## Alternative explanations I must take seriously

Several explanations could account for the pattern without commitment causing better learning.

- **Prior motivation or capability:** Participants who were already more skilled, conscientious, curious, or resourced may both commit more strongly and learn more.
- **Time and life conditions:** Workload, health, family responsibilities, money, safety, and access to support can determine whether a person can continue, regardless of commitment.
- **Project quality and fit:** A clear, meaningful, appropriately difficult project may generate commitment and learning; an ambiguous or imposed project may suppress both.
- **Reverse direction:** Progress may create commitment rather than commitment creating progress.
- **Peer accountability:** Repeated project review and mutual help may explain part of the persistence.
- **Instructor attention:** Participants with visible progress may have received more useful attention, while my expectations may have shaped both interaction and interpretation.
- **Course-access rules:** Requiring a written project for class participation may have generated compliance that I later interpreted as a type of commitment.
- **Selective memory:** This account is partly reconstructed from professional memory rather than a complete contemporaneous dataset.
- **Outcome ambiguity:** Project completion, project quality, course performance, transfer, and durable learning are different outcomes. I did not measure them separately.

These are not footnotes to remove later. They are part of the question.

## What this may imply for leadership education

Even without a causal conclusion, the distinction suggests a practical discipline for educators and learners.

Before adding more content, an educator can create a conversation around questions such as:

1. **What future are you committed to bringing into existence through this project?**
2. **What observable evidence would tell you that it has been realized?**
3. **What are your actions currently organized to complete: the result, an attempt, a plan, approval, proof of competence, or an inquiry into feasibility?**
4. **What does this project make worth learning that would otherwise remain irrelevant?**
5. **When a breakdown occurs, what does it mean inside your current context?**
6. **Which commitment, strategy, relationship, or design now needs revision?**
7. **What ethical or practical boundary would make stopping or pivoting the responsible action?**

A teacher can also separate four things that are often collapsed:

- the future or result;
- the process and next actions;
- the evidence used to assess progress;
- and the identity or image the learner is trying to protect.

This does not replace curriculum, expertise, practice, feedback, assessment, psychological safety, or resources. It may help determine what all of them are serving.

## A stronger way to investigate the question

A future study could treat this observation as the beginning of a testable program rather than its conclusion.

One possible design would:

1. recruit one or more project-based learning cohorts prospectively;
2. record each participant’s project, intended result, and open-ended answer to “What are you committed to?” before substantial instruction;
3. distinguish the **object**, **strength**, and **autonomy** of commitment rather than reducing them to one score;
4. repeat the assessment after major milestones and breakdowns;
5. measure learning separately from project completion, including conceptual understanding, applied performance, delayed retention, and transfer to a different problem;
6. record behaviors such as resource seeking, help seeking, revision, peer contribution, attendance, and time on task;
7. use independent assessors and a predeclared rubric for project quality;
8. compare a commitment-distinction intervention with an appropriate comparison condition;
9. collect interviews to understand how the project occurred for participants, while keeping those reports distinct from behavioral outcomes;
10. predeclare hypotheses, missing-data rules, and alternative explanations;
11. protect participant privacy and make clear that course access or evaluation does not depend on research participation.

A particularly useful result would not be “commitment works.” It would be a more precise account of **which forms of commitment, for whom, under what conditions, through which behaviors, and for which kinds of learning**.

It is also possible that the categories in this note will prove too simple. Planning commitments may be essential for novices. A feasibility commitment may be exactly right in uncertain innovation. Outcome commitments may impair learning when they create defensiveness or escalation of commitment. A careful study should be able to revise the distinction, not merely confirm it.

## My current interpretation

My present interpretation is that learners do not learn only from content. They also learn from the future to which the content has become answerable.

When a project is merely an assignment, a course can remain information. When the project becomes a meaningful future for which the learner accepts authorship, the same course may become equipment for seeing and acting. Commitment may organize attention, define what counts as an unresolved problem, and make inquiry, help, revision, and coordination more available.

The sentence I would currently preserve is:

> **People may not only act in accordance with what they say they want. They often act in accordance with what they have made sufficient to complete.**

That is why the distinction between commitment to realization and commitment to trying, planning, wanting, complying, or appearing active can be so consequential. The latter are not worthless; they become limiting when they can be completed without the future they were supposed to serve.

The educator’s role, then, is not only to transfer knowledge or generate motivation. It may also be to reveal the context in which the learner is already acting, give language to distinctions the learner could not previously see, and return choice to them.

This is still an interpretation. I do not yet know how much of the observed difference belongs to commitment, how much belongs to self-regulation, project meaning, peer relationships, instructor design, or prior capability. I do know that after seeing the distinction, I could no longer treat the question “Are you committed?” as sufficient.

The more responsible question is:

> **What, precisely, are you committed to—and what world does that commitment make available for learning?**

## Why I am publishing an unfinished question

I am not publishing this note to announce a finished theory. I am publishing it because some questions deserve a durable public home before they are solved.

This question has stayed with me across leadership education, coaching, organizational work, and the design of learning systems. I return to it when I read about motivation, goals, self-regulation, attention, identity, and context. Each source gives me another distinction. Each real project exposes a weakness in the interpretation or opens another path.

There is a particular kind of energy—and, for me, a kind of calm—in remaining with a question that emerged from practice and refusing both easy certainty and easy abandonment. Perhaps researchers have already answered parts of it in languages I have not yet connected. Perhaps the categories I observed will need to be divided, renamed, or discarded. That possibility is not a reason to hide the question. It is a reason to make its evidence, limits, and next steps visible.

My hope is that this note can be useful in two ways: immediately, as a distinction available to a learner or educator; and over time, as a record that can be corrected, tested, and developed by better observation and research.
