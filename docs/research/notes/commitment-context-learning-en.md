# What Are Learners Actually Committed To? Commitment as a Context for Learning
{: .no_toc }

{ Working Research Note · Project-based leadership education | fs-6 }

{ [Persian edition / نسخه‌ی فارسی](/research/notes/commitment-context-learning-fa) | rtl }

In project-based leadership programs, I repeatedly saw people take part in broadly the same course in very different ways. Some entered the world of their projects: they searched beyond assigned material, asked sharper questions, used peers and experts as resources, and stayed with breakdowns—obstacles, failed attempts, or missed agreements that interrupted the expected path. Others attended, planned, wrote, and tried, yet the course seemed to remain outside the work they said mattered.

The difference left me with a question:

> **What are learners actually committed to, and how might the object of that commitment shape what becomes available for them to learn?**

{: .note-title }
> Evidence boundary
>
> This is a working field note, not a scientific paper or causal finding. It draws on aggregated observations from several project-based leadership programs, including large cohorts and a year-long group of roughly 30–40 participants. The programs were not controlled studies. I was the facilitator, asked the questions described here, and interpreted what followed. The typology in this note was reconstructed from project forms and facilitator records where available, together with professional memory; the material was not systematically coded, and the prevalence of each category is unknown. There was no random assignment, comparison group, independent assessment, or validated predeclared measure. Participant details are omitted, and the research cited later provides adjacent concepts—not proof of my interpretation.

## The observation behind the question

The programs asked participants to take responsibility for projects, not only discuss leadership. In a smaller year-long group, we met twice a week and repeatedly clarified the projects, reviewed breakdowns, and examined one another's work.

I began asking a deliberately simple question:

> **What is your commitment in relation to this project?**

The question was not a request for an inspiring sentence. In conversation, different answers became visible. One person was answerable for producing the intended result. Another was committed to trying, planning, determining feasibility, completing the course requirement, or demonstrating effort. Some answers contained several layers—planning to try, or preparing to become willing to act.

These positions were often sincere. The important distinction was not between committed and uncommitted people. Almost everyone was committed to something. The question was what their actions treated as sufficient completion.

## Commitment has an object

Planning, trying, and testing feasibility are necessary in serious work. They become limiting when an intermediate activity silently replaces the result it was meant to serve.

I use **object of commitment** to mean the result, activity, status, or inquiry that a person's actions treat as what must actually be completed. The following is an author-generated, provisional field typology. It was not validated, derived through systematic coding, or designed to classify people permanently.

| Operative object of commitment | What can count as completion | Typical meaning of a breakdown |
|---|---|---|
| Realizing a result | Credible evidence that the result exists—or a responsible decision to redesign, pivot, or stop | New information requiring inquiry and choice |
| Trying | A sincere or sufficient effort was made | Evidence that the agreement has been fulfilled |
| Planning | A plan was produced or improved | A reason to return to planning |
| Determining feasibility | A conclusion of “possible” or “not possible” was reached | Completion of the investigation |
| Compliance or access | The required form, proposal, or artifact was submitted | A condition to satisfy |
| Appearing active | Other people can see work or effort | A threat to the desired image |

The categories can overlap. Someone may plan **in order to** realize a result. A feasibility inquiry may be exactly right in uncertain innovation. The distinction becomes useful when the intermediate activity can be completed while the intended future remains untouched.

Strength is therefore only half of the question. A person can be intensely committed to looking competent, following a rule, or proving that a project is impossible. We need to ask both:

1. How strong is the commitment?
2. What is the commitment actually serving?

## What appeared to change when the object changed

My recurring observation was that participants often produced the condition their actions were organized to complete. Those committed to trying usually tried. Those committed to planning often produced plans. Those investigating possibility reached conclusions about possibility. None of these results was necessarily the project.

When some participants shifted toward being answerable for a meaningful result, their relationship with learning appeared to change. I inferred a shift when a participant's language about what counted as done changed and later actions appeared consistent with that new standard. This criterion was not declared in advance or independently coded, and counterexamples were not recorded systematically. The observation is therefore a source of hypotheses, not an estimate of effect or frequency.

The apparent difference was not simply more effort.

- **Attention gained direction.** A concept mattered when it could alter a live project.
- **Questions became consequential.** “What does this mean?” was joined by “What does this reveal, and what action follows?”
- **Resource seeking expanded.** Books, articles, specialists, peers, and difficult conversations became part of the path.
- **Not knowing became easier to admit.** Protecting an image competed less successfully with finding what the project required.
- **Peer learning became operational.** Another person's project could reveal a pattern or option relevant to one's own.
- **Breakdowns generated inquiry.** A failed attempt more often led to questions about assumptions, skills, relationships, strategy, or design.

These are facilitator observations, not measured outcomes. Commitment did not guarantee success, and persistence was not always responsible. Some projects should stop because they are harmful, impossible, unlawful, obsolete, or no longer worth their cost. Responsible commitment is bounded by ethics, health, evidence, relationships, and finite resources.

The distinction is not stubbornness versus quitting. It is whether a breakdown automatically ends responsibility or becomes information for a new choice.

## Commitment as context

The experience led me to think of commitment not only as an internal quantity but as a context: a background from which a situation becomes meaningful.

When a learner was genuinely answerable for a project, the same course could be experienced differently:

- a lecture could occur as equipment for action rather than information to remember;
- confusion as a question rather than evidence of inadequacy;
- a peer as a collaborator rather than a competitor;
- feedback as data rather than a judgment of identity;
- and a breakdown as a design problem rather than a verdict.

In this sense, the learner did not merely “have” a commitment. The learner lived in a world organized by it. The future shaped what became visible in the present: what deserved attention, whom to ask, how long to remain with uncertainty, and what counted as completion.

This language is influenced by ontological and phenomenological approaches to leadership. I use it as an interpretive lens, not as an experimentally established mechanism.

A provisional model is:

> **Object of commitment → context in which the project occurs → attention and standards of completion → inquiry, strategy, help seeking, and coordination → action and opportunities for learning**

The arrows are not one-way. Progress may strengthen commitment; failure may weaken or deepen it; an imposed project may suppress both commitment and learning; instructor attention and peer recognition may change what the learner is trying to complete.

## The educator's role

An educator cannot manufacture another person's commitment by command. A forced declaration can create compliance or impression management. Authorship—and the freedom to revise, refuse, pivot, or stop—must remain with the learner.

The educator can help make the operative commitment visible.

Three practices have become useful in my work:

### Separate the declared goal from what action completes

A learner may declare a result while their pattern of action reliably completes preparation, approval, or proof of effort. The task is not to accuse them of a hidden motive. It is to examine what their current actions make sufficient.

### Create a choice, not a correct answer

Once the distinction is visible, the learner may recommit to the result, narrow it, redefine it, make feasibility the proper inquiry, or leave responsibly. The point is not to prescribe “result commitment” in every situation; it is to restore authorship.

### Revisit the question after breakdowns

Commitment can split, migrate, or become obsolete. A breakdown may call for more knowledge, a new strategy, another relationship, a redesigned project, or an honest completion. The question belongs throughout the work, not only on an opening form.

## A practical conversation

The distinction can be used without turning it into a motivational speech. Before adding more content or demanding more effort, an educator and learner can examine:

1. What future are you willing to be answerable for through this project?
2. What observable evidence would show that the future exists?
3. What do your current actions reliably complete: the result, a plan, an attempt, approval, proof of competence, or a feasibility inquiry?
4. What has become worth learning because of this project?
5. When a breakdown occurs, what does it mean inside your present context?
6. Which assumption, skill, relationship, strategy, or project boundary now needs revision?
7. What ethical or practical condition would make pivoting or stopping the responsible choice?

The conversation should keep four elements separate: the intended future, the next process or action, the evidence used to assess progress, and the identity the learner may be trying to protect. Collapsing them can make feedback on a project feel like a verdict on the person.

These questions do not replace curriculum, expertise, deliberate practice, psychological safety, time, or resources. Nor should they become an instrument for blaming learners whose circumstances constrain action. Their value is diagnostic and generative: they can reveal whether more instruction is actually needed, or whether the current learning system is organized around a different result than everyone assumes.

## What adjacent research adds

Several research traditions help discipline this inquiry without being identical to it.

- **Goal-setting and goal commitment** treat commitment as attachment and determination in relation to a goal. My field question adds a prior issue: which goal is behavior actually serving?
- **Achievement-goal research** distinguishes developing competence, demonstrating competence, and avoiding incompetence. It helps explain why similar activities can be organized by different purposes.
- **Self-regulated learning** describes goal setting, strategy selection, monitoring, help seeking, and adaptation. These resemble behaviors I observed, but direction remains unresolved: commitment may activate self-regulation, self-regulated learners may commit differently, or both may reflect other conditions.
- **Self-determination theory** warns that intensity is not ownership. Strong compliance with an instructor is not the same as an autonomous commitment.
- **Implementation intentions** show that commitment does not replace plans, skills, feedback, and opportunity. A future without implementation remains a declaration.
- **Ontological leadership** offers language for context, occurring, and being answerable to a future. Here it is a conceptual vocabulary, not causal evidence.

Together, these traditions make the original observation more precise and less romantic.

## Alternative explanations and limits

Several factors could produce the pattern without commitment causing better learning:

- participants with more skill, curiosity, conscientiousness, support, or time may both commit differently and learn more;
- a clear and meaningful project may generate commitment rather than result from it;
- progress may increase commitment;
- peer review and accountability may explain persistence;
- visible progress may have attracted more useful instructor attention;
- course requirements may have produced compliance that I interpreted as a form of commitment;
- professional memory may preserve confirming cases more readily;
- project completion, project quality, course performance, retention, and transfer are different outcomes that I did not measure separately.

Life conditions also matter. Workload, health, money, family responsibility, safety, and access to support can determine whether a person continues. Describing every failure as insufficient commitment would be both analytically weak and ethically unfair.

## How the question could be studied

A prospective study could record the intended project result and an open-ended account of commitment before substantial instruction, then distinguish:

- the **object** of commitment;
- its **strength**;
- and its **degree of authorship or autonomy**.

It could measure learning separately from completion through conceptual understanding, applied performance, delayed retention, and transfer. Resource seeking, help seeking, revision, peer contribution, attendance, and time on task could be recorded. Independent assessors could evaluate project quality using a predeclared rubric.

A commitment-distinction conversation could then be compared with an appropriate alternative. Interviews could examine how the project occurred for learners while remaining separate from behavioral outcomes. The design should predeclare hypotheses and missing-data rules, protect privacy, and ensure that access or evaluation does not depend on research participation.

The useful result would not be “commitment works.” It would identify **which forms of commitment, for whom, under what conditions, through which behaviors, and for which kinds of learning**.

## Current interpretation

My present interpretation is that learners do not learn only from content. They also learn from the real future or result for which the content becomes useful.

When a project is merely an assignment, a course can remain information. When a meaningful future becomes something the learner authors and accepts responsibility for, the same course may become equipment for seeing and acting.

The sentence I would preserve is:

> **People may not only act in accordance with what they say they want. They often act in accordance with what they have made sufficient to complete.**

That is why “Are you committed?” is not enough. The more responsible question is:

> **What, precisely, are you committed to—and what does that commitment make worth noticing, learning, and doing?**
