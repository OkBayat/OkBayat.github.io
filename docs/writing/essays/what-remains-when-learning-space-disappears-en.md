# What Remains When the Learning Space Disappears?
{: .no_toc }

{ Performance, learning, transfer, and the capacity to meet a new context | fs-6 }

[نسخه‌ی فارسی](/writing/essays/what-remains-when-learning-space-disappears-fa)

{: .note-title }
> About this essay
>
> This essay combines published scholarship with my professional observations from leadership education, coaching, facilitated groups, and organizational work. The observations are generalized and contain no participant material or identifying client details. They can clarify a question and improve practice, but they do not establish that a program or facilitator caused durable change. Sources support the conceptual distinctions used here; they do not validate my recollections or prove the outcomes of my work.

The room is working too.

When someone begins to speak more honestly in a learning group, takes responsibility in a leadership program, or coordinates better inside a supported team, it is tempting to locate the whole change inside that person. We say that they learned, grew, or transformed.

But the room may be carrying part of the performance. The group may make uncertainty less dangerous. A facilitator may slow the conversation before an old reaction takes over. Shared language may help people name something they could not previously see. Peers may provide attention, memory, encouragement, and accountability. The schedule may protect time that ordinary life does not protect.

Then the course ends. The facilitator leaves. The learner joins another team. Financial pressure rises. A family conflict activates an older history. The language that felt natural inside the program sounds strange elsewhere. Sometimes the new behavior remains. Sometimes it weakens. Sometimes its visible form disappears while a quieter capacity survives.

This does not make the original experience false. It makes the question more demanding:

> **What remains when the learning space disappears—and what would justify calling what remains learning, transfer, or transformation?**

## The problem of environment-dependent change

Human action never occurs outside a context. A person speaks in a relationship, makes decisions inside material constraints, and responds to histories, incentives, roles, expectations, and power. A learning space changes some of those conditions deliberately.

It may create permission to ask questions, a rhythm for reflection, a temporary release from ordinary responsibilities, or a group in which trying something unfamiliar carries less social risk. Under those conditions, a learner may show capacities that were previously unavailable or invisible.

That is a real achievement. A supportive environment can reveal possibility. The mistake is not creating that environment; the mistake is attributing everything that happens within it to a stable change in the learner.

The opposite mistake is equally serious. If a new behavior weakens in a hostile workplace, it does not follow that nothing was learned. The learner may possess a capacity that the new setting makes costly to use. An employee may know how to report an error early and still remain silent under a manager who punishes bad news. A person may have learned to examine assumptions and yet lack the time, safety, or authority to act on what they see.

The disappearance of performance therefore cannot, by itself, tell us whether learning disappeared. It tells us that the relationship between person and environment changed.

This is why the problem should not be framed as a contest between individual responsibility and social context. The useful unit is the relationship between them. We need to ask what the learner can now perceive and do, what the environment permits or rewards, and whether the learner can participate in reshaping that environment.

## Temporary performance, learning, and durable transfer

In ordinary conversation, these terms are often treated as synonyms. They are not.

[Nicholas Soderstrom and Robert Bjork](https://doi.org/10.1177/1745691615569000) distinguish performance—the behavior observable during acquisition or practice—from learning, which is a more durable change that must be inferred after time or changed conditions. Good performance during instruction can create an illusion of learning, while conditions that make practice look more difficult can sometimes support better later retention.

[Susan Barnett and Stephen Ceci](https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-2909.128.4.612) show why transfer requires a more precise question than “Did it work?” Transfer can vary across knowledge domains, physical and social settings, time intervals, purposes, and the distance between the original task and the new one. A person repeating an exercise in the same room is doing something different from recognizing and using its underlying principle months later in an unfamiliar conflict.

The distinction can be summarized this way:

| Outcome | What can be observed | What it can support | What it cannot establish by itself |
|---|---|---|---|
| Temporary performance | A behavior appears during or soon after instruction, facilitation, prompting, or group support | The behavior is possible under those conditions | That the change will remain after support is removed |
| Learning | Knowledge or capability remains available after a delay, even when immediate performance fluctuates | A relatively durable change has occurred | That the capability will be used in a substantially different setting |
| Durable transfer | Learning remains usable, adaptable, or reconstructable across time, tasks, relationships, or settings | The learner can bring something consequential into changed conditions | That the person is independent of context or permanently transformed |

“Transformation” is a stronger claim still. It suggests that more than one response or technique changed: the person’s way of interpreting situations, relating to experience, or participating in the world changed in a consequential and enduring way. A powerful account from a participant may be meaningful evidence of experience, but it is not sufficient on its own to establish such a claim.

Transfer is also not faithful copying. [Vivienne Griggs and colleagues](https://doi.org/10.1080/03075079.2016.1232382) interviewed human-resources professionals six months to three years after formal study of reflective practice. The participants did not simply reproduce classroom techniques. Transfer was uneven, but some accounts suggested that a more questioning relationship to experience had remained even when the visible method had not.

That possibility matters. The most important remainder of a course may not be its vocabulary, worksheet, or sequence of steps. It may be a capacity to pause, notice a pattern, include another perspective, or create a new response. Yet the less visible the outcome becomes, the easier it is to romanticize. We need language precise enough to recognize subtle learning without calling every later success transformation.

## The group, facilitator, language, and wider context

A learning space is not one variable. It is a system of interacting supports and constraints.

### The group

A group can lend courage, memory, attention, and accountability. One member asks the question another avoids. A peer remembers a commitment. Seeing someone admit an error can lower the cost of honesty for everyone else. Through repeated interaction, members may develop norms that make reflection and experimentation ordinary.

But a group can also produce compliance, performance, rivalry, silence, or dependence. [Jean Lave and Etienne Wenger’s account of situated learning](https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9780511815355) places learning within participation in social practice rather than treating it only as the acquisition of portable information. This helps explain both the strength and the limitation of a cohort: people may genuinely become more capable through participation while some of that capability remains entangled with the relationships and practices of that community.

[Rosemary C. Reilly’s arts-based action research](https://doi.org/10.33524/cjar.v22i3.585) at Concordia University also shows that continuity does not automatically make a cohort healthy. Across four graduate cohorts, artistic forms helped make difficult group dynamics available for collective inquiry. The study supports a bounded claim about surfacing and discussing group experience; it does not prove that the intervention caused durable change after the cohort ended.

### The facilitator

A facilitator influences what receives attention, how quickly a group moves, what kind of disagreement is possible, and what happens after someone says, “I do not know.” Even a quiet facilitator is not neutral. Their questions, expectations, authority, and relationship with participants form part of the environment.

Good facilitation can make a capacity available before a learner can sustain it alone. That is often necessary. Scaffolding is not failure. The risk appears when the facilitator becomes the permanent source of reflection, permission, interpretation, or coordination. A group may look autonomous while continuously orienting toward the facilitator’s approval.

The practical question is not whether the facilitator had an effect. They did. It is whether that effect gradually expands the agency of learners and peers—or concentrates the group’s capacity in one central person.

### Language

Language can make distinctions available. Separating an observation from an interpretation, a request from a complaint, or a breakdown from a verdict can change what a person notices and what action becomes possible. Shared terms can help a group remember a practice and coordinate around it.

Language can also become borrowed fluency. A learner may repeat the vocabulary of a program while continuing to act from an older pattern. Specialized language may even stop transfer if it can only be spoken among insiders.

The more durable achievement is translation: can the learner recognize the principle when the familiar phrase is absent, express it in language another community can use, and adapt it without losing its substance? If the words disappear but the distinction remains active, something may have travelled. If the words remain but choice does not change, less may have travelled than the fluent performance suggests.

### The wider context

The context outside the learning space includes managers, family relationships, workload, tools, economic pressure, cultural expectations, authority, incentives, and opportunities to practise. Classic work on training transfer by [Timothy Baldwin and J. Kevin Ford](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1744-6570.1988.tb00632.x), and a later review by [Rebecca Grossman and Eduardo Salas](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1468-2419.2011.00373.x), organize transfer around the interaction of learner characteristics, training design, and the work environment.

This is a useful correction to the idea that a sufficiently motivated person should transfer learning anywhere. Agency matters, but an organization can systematically punish the behavior its training celebrates. Sending people to a workshop on candour while rewarding concealment is not a learner defect. It is a contradiction in organizational design.

## What I have observed in professional practice

This question did not begin for me in the literature. It began with repeated discomfort in practice.

In leadership programs and facilitated groups, I have seen people ask questions more freely, acknowledge mistakes earlier, and take greater responsibility while a particular social container was present. I have also seen some of those patterns weaken when the person returned to a group with a different history, authority structure, or cost for speaking honestly. The return could look abrupt, almost like a reset.

In organizational work, I have seen teams coordinate differently while a founder, manager, or facilitator was actively holding the context. Once that central figure stepped back, it became clearer whether agreements, reflection, and responsibility had become distributed practices or were still being supplied by one person.

My coaching experience gave me another distinction. Helping someone solve one immediate problem is not the same as helping them recognize a reusable response. Recognizing a reusable response is not the same as seeing the recurring assumptions and interpretations through which a family of problems keeps appearing. The deeper the learning claim, the more important it becomes to observe what happens when the original problem and conversation are no longer present.

I have also seen signs that something can remain without the original form. Some people stop using the program’s exact language but continue to pause before reacting, examine their part in a pattern, or look for an available action after a setback. In such cases, the method may have faded while a way of relating to difficulty remained.

The practice records on this site keep these observations deliberately bounded. [Learning Circle](/leadership-learning/human-transformation/field-projects/learning-circle) records peer support and early coordination during the directly observed phase, but does not claim durable autonomy after the facilitator’s regular presence ended. [Mastery for Life](/leadership-learning/human-transformation/practice-programs/mastery-for-life) preserves participant reports and professional observations while stating that they do not establish causality, generalizability, or permanent transformation. An exploratory [field note](/research/notes/what-remains-when-the-course-ends) and a source-based [inquiry into reflective practice](/research/notes/can-the-learner-carry-the-context) document how this question developed.

The most responsible conclusion I can draw from my experience is limited: **a learning environment and a learner can jointly produce a change that neither would produce in the same way alone.** Whether the learner can later recover, adapt, or recreate the relevant conditions is a different question from whether the original experience mattered.

## Alternative explanations and limits

Professional observation is especially vulnerable to attractive explanations. If a former participant appears more reflective or persistent, the program may not be the reason. People select themselves into longer or more demanding programs. Motivation, age, experience, income, family support, health, opportunity, and concurrent events may differ. People change over time without an intervention, and a difficult period may pass on its own.

Participant reports can be influenced by memory, gratitude, loyalty, current identity, or the wish to give the facilitator a satisfying answer. Facilitators remember vivid cases and can overlook quiet, neutral, contradictory, or unsuccessful experiences. When the same person teaches, observes, interprets, and writes, role conflict and expectancy are unavoidable concerns.

The published literature does not remove these limits. Laboratory research on memory, workplace training studies, professional-education interviews, situated-learning theory, and action research in cohorts examine different populations, tasks, settings, and outcomes. Their concepts can sharpen interpretation, but findings cannot be transferred carelessly from one domain to another.

There is also no context-free test of a context-free human being. A person who acts differently in a new environment has not escaped context; they are participating in another one. Durable transfer should therefore not mean independence from relationships or conditions. A more credible meaning is that the learner has greater capacity to recognize a new context, preserve or adapt what matters, seek support, and influence the conditions around them.

Nothing in this essay demonstrates:

- that one course, coaching model, or facilitator causes durable transformation;
- that visible continuity is always evidence of deep learning;
- that a temporary return to an older pattern means all learning was lost;
- that the same design works across cultures, ages, organizations, or kinds of learning;
- or that individuals should be blamed for failing to overcome structurally unsafe conditions.

These limits do not empty the inquiry of value. They prevent a meaningful experience from being turned into a claim larger than the evidence.

## Practical implications

### For the learner

Treat the learning space as both an opportunity and a source of support that should become visible.

- Ask which parts of your current performance depend on prompts, people, schedules, or language supplied by the environment.
- Revisit an idea after time has passed and in a setting that does not provide the original cues.
- Translate a distinction into ordinary language and apply it to a problem that does not resemble the classroom example.
- Notice the return of an older pattern without treating it as proof of failure; it may reveal which conditions still govern your action.
- Build reflective relationships and practices in the new context instead of imagining that mature learning means needing no support.

The aim is not self-sufficiency. It is greater authorship in how support is recognized, chosen, and created.

### For the facilitator

Design the learning space so that your presence is useful but not indispensable.

- Make the supports visible: name what the group, rhythm, prompts, and shared language are contributing.
- Let participants question, translate, and sometimes reject the program’s vocabulary rather than rewarding verbal imitation.
- Move responsibility gradually from facilitator to peers and from protected exercises to varied situations.
- Create opportunities to practise after prompts are reduced, not only while support is at its strongest.
- Speak modestly about outcomes: an intense experience, participant testimony, and excellent end-of-course performance are not yet evidence of durable transfer.

A facilitator succeeds not only when the room becomes powerful, but when learners become more capable of building reflective conditions without reproducing dependence on the facilitator.

### For the organization

Do not purchase training while preserving a system that makes its use irrational.

- Align management behavior, incentives, workload, and consequences with the capacities the organization says it wants.
- Give people real opportunities to use new judgment, not only permission in principle.
- Build peer practices, feedback routines, and shared language into work rather than locating development only in occasional courses.
- Examine whether authority figures can receive questions, errors, and disagreement without punishment.
- Distinguish a training problem from a structural problem. A workshop cannot compensate indefinitely for unsafe leadership, contradictory incentives, or a lack of decision authority.

The organization is not merely the destination to which learning transfers. It becomes part of what is learned, what can be expressed, and what remains possible.

## Conclusion: what may remain

When the learning space disappears, the most obvious things may go first: the schedule, exercises, group rituals, facilitator’s questions, and shared phrases.

What remains may be less theatrical. A pause before an automatic reaction. The ability to distinguish what happened from the story built around it. A willingness to ask for another perspective. A capacity to notice that the new room is shaping behavior. The judgment to seek support without surrendering authorship. The ability to help a group create enough safety, language, and responsibility to learn together.

None of these capacities makes a person independent of context. Perhaps that was never the right ambition. The stronger form of learning may be a changed relationship with context: the learner can see more of what the environment is doing, recover what matters, translate it, and participate in creating conditions in which responsible action becomes possible again.

The test of a learning space is therefore not only what happens inside it.

It is what the learner can recognize, carry, adapt, and help rebuild after the room is gone.
