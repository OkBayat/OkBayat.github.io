# Can Awareness Open Space for Change? A Retrospective Practitioner Reflection
{: .no_toc }

{ Essay · Retrospective Practitioner Reflection | fs-6 }

For years, one question has followed me through leadership education, facilitation, coaching, and ordinary relationships:

> **What becomes possible when a person can see a recurring pattern without immediately being judged, diagnosed, or told how to change it?**

I did not begin with a research protocol. I began as a practitioner designing exercises that invited people to notice inner dialogue, responses to situations, or visible commitments over time. The experiences changed how I facilitate and sharpened a question I still want to study. They did not, however, give me ethical or methodological grounds to treat participants' past messages, written reflections, images, or classroom records as research data for public analysis.

This essay therefore does something narrower. It reflects on my own practice: what I was trying to create, where my interpretation became too ambitious, what I now do differently, and how a future study would need to be designed from the beginning.

{: .note-title }
> Ethical boundary
>
> This is not a field-study report and it does not evaluate former participants. I do not quote, reconstruct, summarize, or analyze anyone's private writing, messages, images, relationships, or personal decisions. Educational participation and trust in an instructor were not consent to later public research. The only subject I can responsibly examine here is my own evolving practice and the question it produced.

## Where the question came from

An earlier inquiry had led me to distinguish what learners say they want from what their actions are organized to complete. A person may say that they are committed to a result while repeatedly completing plans, attempts, preparation, or proof of effort. That distinction raised another question for me: was a new commitment producing change, or did change sometimes begin because a person could finally see the pattern already organizing their action?

In practice, I experimented with simple structures for observation. Some involved recording thoughts or responses across ordinary situations. Others made a limited set of visible commitments easier to review. The details varied, but the intention was similar: record something concrete, delay interpretation, look for repetition, and allow the person to decide what—if anything—the pattern meant.

I was interested in whether a mirror could sometimes be more useful than advice.

## Awareness is not information

Knowing a definition is different from recognizing oneself in a live pattern. A person can understand concepts such as defensiveness, avoidance, approval seeking, or commitment for years without noticing how one of them appears in a particular conversation.

By awareness, I mean a practical moment of recognition: a recurring response becomes observable as a response rather than disappearing into “this is simply who I am” or “that is just how the situation is.”

This definition is deliberately modest. Awareness here is not a validated psychological construct, a neurological event, or evidence of transformation. It is a name for seeing a pattern with enough specificity that another relationship to it may become possible.

The structures I used shared four features:

1. they began with a concrete event, statement, or action rather than a personality label;
2. they repeated observation across time rather than relying on one dramatic incident;
3. they delayed moral judgment and explanation;
4. they left interpretation and choice, as far as possible, with the person doing the observing.

That last feature became central to my practice. If I tell someone, “You are an anxious person” or “You always avoid responsibility,” I have not created awareness; I have offered a diagnosis that the person can accept, resist, or perform for me. A mirror should make something inspectable without claiming ownership of its meaning.

## The mirror is still an intervention

It would be misleading to describe these practices as “awareness alone.” A prompt directs attention. A notebook changes what can be remembered. Repetition creates a practice. A group establishes norms. A facilitator carries authority. A table can create accountability or pressure.

The mirror is not neutral simply because it contains no advice.

Writing may organize experience. Measurement may alter behavior. Seeing that others participate may create belonging, conformity, or self-presentation. A facilitator's expectations may shape what is noticed and what remains invisible. Public visibility can produce obedience rather than self-authored choice.

This matters because I once found the language of awareness so compelling that it could make a complex process appear simple: people see a pattern and begin to change. A more responsible description is that structured observation may interact with writing, attention, repetition, relationship, safety, accountability, existing skill, and the surrounding environment. Without a prospective design, those influences cannot be separated.

## Conceptual touchstones, not retrospective proof

Several bodies of work help me frame this inquiry, but they do not validate my recollections or prove that the earlier exercises caused change.

[Donald Schön's work on reflective practice](https://www.hachettebookgroup.com/titles/donald-a-schon/the-reflective-practitioner/9780465068784/) offers language for examining professional judgment while acting and after action. [Barry Zimmerman's overview of self-regulated learning](https://doi.org/10.1207/S15430421TIP4102_2) treats self-observation as one part of a larger cycle that also includes goals, strategy, self-evaluation, and adaptation. [Richard Ryan and Edward Deci's account of self-determination theory](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cedpsych.2020.101860) makes the difference between autonomy support and control especially relevant when a facilitator introduces a reflective structure. And [Jim McCambridge and colleagues' systematic review](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jclinepi.2013.08.015) cautions that observation and research participation can themselves affect behavior, while the mechanisms and magnitudes of those effects remain difficult to establish.

I use these sources as lenses for refining questions and design choices. None of them shows what happened in my earlier groups, and none turns participant material collected for education into research data.

## Where retrospective interpretation reaches its limit

The earlier version of this page described several educational experiences as field observations and discussed patterns in participant writing, follow-up responses, and classroom records. Names and identifying details were removed, but anonymization did not solve the central ethical problem.

Those materials were created inside educational and facilitation relationships. Participants could reasonably understand them as part of a course or group practice, not as material for a later public inquiry. My roles as designer, instructor, observer, and sometimes evaluator also created a power imbalance. A person may participate, submit a reflection, or speak positively because the instructor is present—not because they freely consent to public analysis.

Reconstructing or combining examples reduces identifiability, but it also introduces more interpretation. It can make a story sound representative without showing how many people had a different experience, stopped participating, felt discomfort, or saw no benefit. Retrospective memory further privileges vivid and confirming cases.

For those reasons, this revision removes the participant-level analysis rather than trying to rescue it with stronger disclaimers. The experiences remain part of my professional history and the origin of my question. They are not presented as a dataset.

## What changed in my practice

The most durable result of these experiences is a change in how I respond when I think I see a pattern.

### I ask permission before moving into reflection

A colleague, friend, learner, or family member may want an ordinary conversation, not an unsolicited coaching session. I now try to ask whether the person wants to examine what I noticed. If the answer is no—or if interest fades—I stop.

### I describe observations before offering an interpretation

Instead of declaring what a person “is,” I can describe the limited events available to me:

> “I may be seeing a pattern, and my interpretation may be incomplete. In several recent conversations, when this subject appeared, the conversation ended quickly. Is that repetition visible to you too?”

The wording matters. It distinguishes the observable sequence from my explanation and makes disagreement legitimate.

### I use fewer questions

When a person becomes interested, one or two questions may be enough:

- Where else, if anywhere, do you notice this?
- What becomes possible if you observe it without trying to correct it immediately?

The pause afterward is part of the work. Filling it with my theory can replace the person's discovery with my performance as a coach.

### I prefer private, owned records

If a person chooses to keep a log, the record should be theirs by default. Public display needs a specific purpose, genuine choice, limited access, and no hidden link to approval, punishment, or status. A participant should be able to decline without losing educational opportunity.

### I treat the structure as temporary

A tool is useful only while it supports seeing and choice. If responsibility disappears the moment a table or facilitator is removed, the structure may have produced dependence or compliance rather than learning. The scaffold should become lighter when it can.

## Awareness is not sufficient for change

A person can see a pattern and continue it. They may lack skill, safety, resources, support, opportunity, or a meaningful alternative. The pattern may provide protection or short-term value. It may be embedded in an organization, family, or economic condition that individual reflection cannot change.

Awareness can also be painful. It may bring shame, rumination, anxiety, or helplessness rather than choice. In contexts involving trauma, addiction, violence, severe distress, or risk of harm, a reflective exercise is not a substitute for qualified professional support.

My current view is therefore not that awareness causes transformation. It is that accurate, non-coercive recognition may sometimes open a small distance between a person and an automatic response. Whether that distance becomes useful depends on commitment, skill, relationship, environment, and action.

I now understand the relationship this way:

> **Awareness may make the present pattern visible; commitment can establish a direction; and action tests whether another possibility can live in the world.**

None of the three guarantees the others.

## What a responsible future study would require

If this question becomes a formal study, the ethical and methodological design must precede data collection.

A responsible design would:

1. define a narrow question and observable outcomes before the exercise begins;
2. obtain informed consent through a process separate from course access, grading, employment, or the facilitator's approval;
3. make nonparticipation and withdrawal possible without penalty;
4. keep personal records private by default and specify who can access them, for how long, and for what purpose;
5. collect only material necessary for the declared question;
6. distinguish self-report, direct behavior, facilitator interpretation, and independent assessment;
7. include baseline and follow-up measures, attrition, missing data, neutral cases, and adverse experiences;
8. compare structured observation with plausible alternatives such as neutral writing, direct feedback, or no exercise;
9. use independent assessors where interpretation is required;
10. offer participants a practical process for correction, removal, and review of any identifiable use.

The more precise question is no longer “Does awareness transform people?” It is:

> **Awareness of what, for whom, under which conditions, through what form of observation, with what support, and for what kind and duration of change is useful?**

That question can produce evidence capable of correcting the practitioner's preferred story.

## A provisional conclusion

I still value the image of a mirror, but I understand its responsibility differently now. A useful mirror must be accurate enough to inspect, limited enough not to become a diagnosis, safe enough not to force exposure, and temporary enough not to create dependence.

My role is not to change another person or to prove that my interpretation is right. It may be to create conditions in which something can become visible while the person's agency remains intact.

That is a smaller public claim than the one I once wanted to make. It is also a more honest foundation for the work ahead.
